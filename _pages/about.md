I am a researcher in the [Salzman Lab](https://zuckermaninstitute.columbia.edu/) at Columbia University's Zuckerman Institute, where I work with Dr. Evan Kyzar on the neurobiology of alcohol use disorder.

Brain regions do not function in a vacuum. Alcohol withdrawal and the behaviors that accompany it are mediated by large-scale, brain-wide coordination of activity rather than by any single region acting alone, and single-timepoint cFos immunohistochemistry is not sensitive enough to resolve those patterns. My work instead uses two-timepoint neural activity labeling with subtraction (TTP-S), which tags the neurons active at two separate moments within the same brain so that overlapping and divergent populations can be told apart. Applied across whole brains, this has identified more than 100 regions consistently activated during alcohol withdrawal, including ensembles in the central amygdala whose activity is strikingly negatively correlated with the rest of the activated network, and it resolves intermingled neuronal populations that respond differently to withdrawal and to intoxication, which may mediate the behavioral switch between those states. I pair the imaging with automated behavioral phenotyping in the elevated plus maze and open field test, correlating what an animal is doing directly with whole-brain activation patterns. Day to day the work involves immunohistochemistry, confocal microscopy, chemogenetics, whole-brain reconstruction, two-bottle choice, conditioned place preference and conditioned taste aversion assays, and a great deal of image analysis.

Before Columbia I worked in the Becher Lab at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, investigating RBPJ-SIX1 as a tumor suppressor axis in diffuse intrinsic pontine glioma. I built mouse models of DIPG and characterized tumor growth using RNA extraction, Western blots, immunofluorescence, and MRI. As an undergraduate at Fordham I directed three independent projects in Dr. Eduardo Gallo's lab on ventral pallidum cholinergic projection neurons in cue-driven motivated behavior, using in vivo fiber photometry.

Alongside the bench, I am Co-Director of Research at Nucleate NY, where I lead scientific and commercial due diligence on early-stage life science ventures for the Activator program. I also mentor high school students through BioBus's Harlem Women in STEM Junior Scientist program at the Zuckerman Institute, and previously helped plan community neuroscience events with Columbia University Neuroscience Outreach.

I am completing a Master of Arts in Biotechnology at Columbia University, expected in October 2027.
